# Ethics and Responsible Use

This project analyzes public election-affidavit disclosures for research and civic accountability. It should be used carefully.

## Principles

1. **Do not treat anomaly scores as findings.**
   A high score means a record deserves review. It does not prove wrongdoing.

2. **Verify before publishing.**
   Check original affidavits, Election Commission records, court filings, company records, land records, and other primary sources.

3. **Respect source terms.**
   Review MyNeta/ADR and other source-site terms before automated collection or redistribution.

4. **Avoid bulk redistribution.**
   Share code, methodology, aggregate results, and research questions. Do not publish full scraped datasets unless permission is obtained.

5. **Be careful with names.**
   Politicians and candidates are public figures, but allegations can still cause harm if unsupported. Use language such as "disclosure anomaly", "public allegation", "manual review lead", and "open question".

6. **Separate data issues from substantive issues.**
   Parser errors, fuzzy-match mistakes, name collisions, missing income disclosures, and constituency changes can all create false positives.

## Recommended Language

Use:

- "financial disclosure anomaly"
- "triage score"
- "manual review queue"
- "requires document-level verification"
- "publicly reported allegation"

Avoid:

- "scam detector"
- "corrupt candidate"
- "proof"
- "fraud confirmed"

## Publication Checklist

Before publishing a case study:

- confirm candidate identity across years;
- confirm values against source affidavits;
- identify whether criminal cases are political/protest-related or financial/property-related;
- verify company links through filings;
- verify land/property claims through official records;
- give the subject's public response where available;
- distinguish allegation, investigation, charge, and judicial finding.
